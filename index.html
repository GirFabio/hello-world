My research interests lie at the intersection between financial economics and decision theory.
I focus on how different sources of uncertainty affect the optimal decisions and compensation of investors on financial markets.

Working Papers

Ambiguity, Prudence and Optimal Portfolio

Abstract: Ambiguity prudence captures an aversion towards model uncertainty when an investor believes that unfavourable outcomes are more likely to realize. I investigate the contribution of ambiguity prudence to the optimal stock allocation when the investor evaluates portfolio compositions as described by the smooth model under ambiguity criterion. I derive a third-order approximation of the certainty equivalent to disentangle the contribution of preference and beliefs. I analyse two relevant portfolio problems to show that ambiguity prudence puts non-linearities into the investor's valuation that induce sizeable variations from the robust mean-variance solution of Maccheroni et al. (2013). These effects emerge because of the willingness to hedge against downside model uncertainty.

Consumption Sharing under Heterogeneous Beliefs and Ambiguity Attitudes

Abstract: This paper studies the optimal consumption allocation when investors have heterogeneous beliefs and ambiguity attitudes towards three sources of uncertainty: the first two concern the conditional distribution of consumption, while the third one concerns the persistence of the shocks hitting the economy. I provide a mechanism to explain why, as economic conditions deteriorate, subjective beliefs polarize. If the economy has a large number of periods, then disagreement over the persistent component of consumption becomes a main driver of the changes in the distribution of wealth.
