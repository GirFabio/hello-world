My research interests lie in the intersection between decision theory and financial economics.
I focus on how risk and ambiguity affect optimal decisions of individual agents and, at the aggregate level, how financial markets compensate investors to bear different types of uncertainty.

Working Papers

"Ambiguity, Prudence and Optimal Portfolio"

Abstract: Recent experimental evidence suggests that ambiguity prudence plays an important role in decision-making. In this work, I investigate the contribution of ambiguity prudence when an investor evaluates portfolio compositions according to the Smooth ambiguity criterion. I derive a third-order approximation of the certainty equivalent that disentangles prudence effects among beliefs and behavioural attitudes. I analyse two relevant portfolio problems to show that, when the certainty equivalents implied by models under evaluation are significantly different, ambiguity prudence might induce sizeable non-linearities in the optimal stock allocations. "Consumption Sharing under Heterogeneous Beliefs and Ambiguity Attitudes"

Abstract: This paper studies the optimal consumption allocation when investors have heterogeneous beliefs and ambiguity attitudes towards three sources of uncertainty: the first two concern the conditional distribution of consumption, while the third one concerns the persistence of the shocks hitting the economy. I provide a mechanism to explain why, as economic conditions deteriorate, subjective beliefs polarize. If the economy has a large number of periods, then disagreement over the persistent component of consumption becomes a main driver of the changes in the distribution of wealth.
